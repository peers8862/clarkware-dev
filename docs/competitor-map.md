# Clark Competitor Map

## Purpose

This document consolidates CLARK's current competitor and analog mapping into one source-of-truth file. It is intended to separate direct competitors, overlapping analogs, benchmarks, and substitutes so investor and strategy language stays disciplined.

## How To Use

- Use this file when describing competitor categories or analogs.
- Treat analogs as overlap references, not proof that CLARK has no competition.
- Keep stronger white-space claims out of investor-facing materials until they are validated more fully.
- Update this file before promoting new comparative language into `docs/pitch.md` or other outward-facing materials.

## Core Framing

CLARK's current competitor set is best understood as overlapping analogs rather than direct equivalents. The strongest safe claim is not that CLARK has no competitors at all, but that its intended combination of distributed node structure, precision assembly, firmware support, workforce training, and facility-aware operational software is unusual.

## Company Profiles

### MacroFab

- Category: Distributed manufacturing platform
- Why it matters: MacroFab is the strongest current analog for distributed manufacturing coordination.
- Overlap with CLARK:
  - distributed network logic
  - digital workflow and sourcing coordination
  - manufacturing-platform framing
- Main differences from CLARK:
  - current CLARK thesis includes firmware optimization, training, and node-ownership logic
  - CLARK is pursuing a more explicit node-network and corridor model
  - CLARK includes facility-aware operational software as part of the platform layer
- Safe public wording:
  - MacroFab is the strongest distributed-manufacturing-platform analog identified so far.

### STI Electronics

## Safe Distinctions Versus CLARK

These distinctions are currently safe enough to use carefully:

- CLARK is pursuing a coordinated node network rather than a single-facility strategy.
- CLARK combines assembly, firmware support, and workforce training in one platform thesis.
- CLARK intends software to be part of the operating layer, not just an internal support tool.
- CLARK's Hamilton-Buffalo corridor thesis gives it a different geographic and operating narrative than single-region analogs.

## Claims That Should Not Yet Be Used Publicly

Do not use these claims yet:

- CLARK has no competitors.
- CLARK is the only company in the market with overlapping capabilities.
- Hamilton-Buffalo is clearly the best corridor because competitors cannot match it.
- Clarkware already creates a proven moat versus all adjacent software categories.

## Recommended Public Language

Use wording like:

- CLARK sits at an unusual intersection of distributed manufacturing, firmware-aware support, workforce training, and facility-aware operational software.
- The closest current analogs overlap with one or two parts of the model rather than the full intended combination.
- MacroFab, STI Electronics, EPTAC, and Conclusive Engineering are the strongest current analog references by category.

Avoid wording like:

- no one else does this
- there are no real competitors
- fully uncontested market position

### Revenue Takeaway

CLARK's revenue model is structurally different because it is intended to combine:

- training-first launch revenue
- node operating revenue
- platform fees for software, onboarding, logistics, training, and IP
- minority equity upside and later capital recycling

That mix makes CLARK closer to a platform-plus-node model than to any single service-provider analog.

## Clarkware Software-Layer Comparison

Clarkware should not be compared only to manufacturing companies. Investors may also compare it to adjacent industrial software categories.

| Software Category | Example Comparison Set | What They Do Well | Where Clarkware Is Different |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Industrial application platforms | Ignition and similar platforms | Device connectivity, reporting, industrial app architecture | Clarkware is more centered on workstation context, notes, messaging, and operational memory than on SCADA/HMI-style platform breadth |
| Frontline operations platforms | Tulip and similar connected-app systems | Frontline workflows, operator apps, device-aware workflows | Clarkware is more explicitly tied to auditable job-linked messaging, evidence continuity, and CLARK's node model |
| IIoT platforms | ThingWorx and similar platforms | Products, people, and process connectivity | Clarkware is narrower and more workstation-centered in version 1 |
| Connected-worker / industrial AI | Augmentir and similar systems | AI-assisted frontline support and workflow guidance | Clarkware is stricter about reviewability and ties AI to a broader operational record |
| Team chat / enterprise messaging | Slack, Teams, Mattermost, Rocket.Chat | Fast communication and broad organizational adoption | Clarkware ties communication to jobs, workstations, evidence, and issue context rather than generic team messaging |
| MES / plant execution systems | Conventional MES vendors | Production tracking, routing, and manufacturing control | Clarkware version 1 is not trying to be a full MES replacement and should not be sold that way |

### Software Comparison Takeaway

Clarkware is strongest when described as a facility-aware operational memory and coordination layer. It becomes weaker if it is described as merely another messaging tool, another dashboard, or a complete MES replacement.

## Buyer-Side Substitute Map

From a buyer perspective, CLARK competes not only against named companies but also against substitute approaches.

| Buyer Need | Common Substitute | Why Buyers Choose It | Why CLARK May Win |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Low-volume industrial assembly | Existing EMS vendor or quick-turn assembler | Familiar supplier, immediate quote path | CLARK aims to combine regional execution, firmware-aware support, training depth, and platform coordination |
| Firmware-sensitive manufacturing support | Separate firmware consultancy plus manufacturer | Specialized expertise by function | CLARK aims to reduce coordination loss between embedded work and production execution |
| Workforce development | External training provider only | Known credential provider, easy procurement | CLARK ties training to real manufacturing context and future node capability development |
| Production coordination | Email, spreadsheets, exported test files, and generic chat | Low switching cost, already in use | Clarkware aims to create traceable operational memory and faster issue resolution |
| Facility reporting | Manual dashboards and supervisor synthesis | Familiar, no new system required | Clarkware aims to derive reporting directly from recorded operational activity |
| Remote expert review | Ad hoc calls, screen shares, and email attachments | Flexible and already available | Clarkware aims to make remote participation contextual, bounded, and auditable |

### Substitute Takeaway

The most important competition in early sales may be inertia rather than a single named rival. CLARK must therefore prove that the integrated model is operationally better than the common patchwork of incumbent vendors, generic communication tools, and manual reporting.

## Investor Comparison Risk

Investors may incorrectly compare CLARK against the wrong category unless the framing is explicit.

Common incorrect comparisons:

- a generic EMS roll-up
- an industrial SaaS company with no operating footprint
- a pure training business
- a generic industrial AI company
- a full-stack factory operating system replacing everything at once

Recommended correction:

- CLARK is a platform company for independently operated industrial nodes.
- Clarkware is the facility-aware software layer of that platform.
- The business is neither pure software nor pure contract manufacturing.
- The right comparison is a hybrid of node network, industrial services, training, and operational software.
